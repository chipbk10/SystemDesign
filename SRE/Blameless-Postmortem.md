A blameless postmortem (or post-incident review) is a structured review conducted after a system outage, software bug, or project failure. Instead of looking for someone to punish, it assumes everyone acted with good intentions based on the information they had, focusing instead on fixing the flawed systems and processes.

## Key Principles

* System Focus: The investigation asks why the system allowed the mistake to happen, rather than who made it.
* Psychological Safety: Removing the fear of reprimand encourages engineers to be fully honest, resulting in a more accurate and thorough analysis.
* Not Consequence-Free: While no individual is shamed or fired, teams are held responsible for implementing the necessary changes to prevent recurrence.

## Standard Structure
A typical blameless postmortem document includes:

   1. Summary: A brief overview of what broke and the impact it caused.
   2. Timeline: The exact sequence of events from when the incident started to when it was resolved.
   3. Root Cause Analysis (RCA): The underlying systemic reasons for the failure.
   4. Action Items: Specific, assigned tasks to prevent the issue from happening again.

## Why Teams Use It
Widely popularized by the Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) community at tech giants like Google and Etsy, this practice transforms costly mistakes into valuable learning opportunities. It builds a transparent, high-trust engineering culture where teams consistently improve system resilience over time.
